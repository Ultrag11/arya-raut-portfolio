Data Triangulation and Human Experience

Dr. Rohit Karthikeyan’s lecture helped me see data triangulation as a way to understand human experience with more humility, not as a way to force people into one perfect metric.

In my ISyE 552 class, Human Factors in Design and Evaluation, we had a guest lecture by Dr. Rohit Karthikeyan on data triangulation. His lecture stood out to me because it was not only technical but also deeply philosophical. Rather than treating human factors as a field where every answer can be reduced to a single metric, he emphasized that human experience is complex, biased, contextual, and often impossible to fully understand from a single data source.

One concept that stayed with me was umwelt, the specific way each person perceives and experiences the world. Every user has a different umwelt, shaped by their background, expectations, environment, biases, and goals. This matters in design because people do not interact with systems as neutral machines. They interpret, assume, hesitate, adapt, and make decisions based on their lived experience.

This is where data triangulation becomes powerful. In human factors, triangulation is not about finding one perfect, deterministic truth. Instead, it is about combining different sources of evidence to better understand a bounded reality. A survey may tell us what a user felt. A sensor may show what happened physically. An observer may notice behaviors the user was unaware of. Interviews may reveal intent, confusion, or meaning behind an action. Each method gives only part of the picture, but together they help reduce ambiguity.

Dr. Karthikeyan emphasized that humans are stochastic, not deterministic. In engineering, we often want clean inputs, predictable outputs, and repeatable solutions. But human behavior does not always work that way. People respond differently depending on context, stress, experience, attention, confidence, and perception. Therefore, human factors should not force deterministic explanations onto human behavior. Instead, it should use probabilistic thinking to understand patterns, tendencies, and possibilities.

One of my biggest takeaways was the idea: solve the experience, not the human. A poor user experience is not always caused by a person making a mistake. It may stem from unclear instructions, mismatched mental models, poor feedback, environmental pressure, or a system that does not support natural human behavior. Good design does not try to “fix” people. It creates systems that better fit the way people actually think, decide, and act.

The lecture also reframed how I think about bias. Bias is not just an error to remove; it is part of how people frame and interpret reality. Subjective experience is influenced by bias, memory, emotion, and perception. Objective data may provide precise measurements, but they may miss meaning. Observational data can reveal actions users do not consciously report. Triangulation helps bring these different perspectives together, enabling designers to search for coherence across imperfect sources of truth.

Dr. Karthikeyan also shared a personal reflection on luck that I found meaningful. He described luck not as pure fortune, but as something connected to effort. If something good happens without effort, that is fortune. But luck often appears when effort, preparation, and opportunity meet. That perspective made the lecture feel broader than just research methods. It connected how we study users with how we approach growth, work, and uncertainty.

For me, this lecture changed how I understand data in human factors. Data is not just numbers in a spreadsheet. It can be subjective, objective, observational, contextual, and interpretive. No single method captures the full truth of a human experience. But by carefully combining multiple sources, we can design better systems, ask better questions, and understand users with greater humility.

The biggest lesson I took from Dr. Karthikeyan’s lecture was that human factors is not about forcing humans into perfect models. It is about recognizing complexity, respecting different perspectives, and using multiple forms of evidence to design experiences that work better for real people.
