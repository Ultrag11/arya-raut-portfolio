Engineering Between the Runway and the Shop Floor

I have always been drawn to machines that move with purpose. Aircraft and vehicles make that feeling obvious, but I have learned that the same kind of beauty exists inside a well-designed production system.

When I worked with DJS Skylark, every decision had a consequence. A change in structure could affect weight. A change in payload could affect stability. A change in planform could affect mission performance. Nothing existed in isolation, and that is exactly what made the project exciting.

Manufacturing has taught me a similar lesson. A production line is also a moving system. If one station waits, another station feels it. If a fixture is awkward, quality and ergonomics both suffer. If the standard work is unclear, the process depends too much on memory and too little on design.

That connection is why I like industrial engineering. It lets me combine my aerospace curiosity with practical process improvement. I can look at a shop floor the way I look at an aircraft: as a system of constraints, tradeoffs, feedback, and performance.

The best engineering work, to me, is not just clever. It is usable. It helps people do safer work, helps teams make better decisions, and turns complexity into something that can actually be operated.
