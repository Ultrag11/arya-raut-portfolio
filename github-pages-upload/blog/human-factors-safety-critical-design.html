Human Factors Reflection

Human Factors, Medical Devices, and Safety-Critical Design

A guest lecture by Dr. Jackie Cha helped me see human factors as a responsibility-driven discipline: designing systems that anticipate, prevent, and manage errors before they harm people.

In my ISyE 552 class, Human Factors in Design and Evaluation, we had a guest lecture by Dr. Jackie Cha, whose work focuses on human factors, healthcare systems, and the interaction among people, technology, and safety-critical environments. Her lecture helped me see that human factors is not just about making products easier to use but about designing systems where errors can be anticipated, prevented, and managed before they harm people.

One of the most interesting parts of the lecture was her explanation of the FDA approval process for medical devices. Before this lecture, I understood FDA regulation only at a high level, but she clarified an important distinction: FDA Approved and FDA Cleared are not the same thing. “FDA Approved” generally applies to higher-risk products that must undergo a more rigorous review process, while “FDA Cleared” typically means the device has demonstrated substantial equivalence to an already legally marketed device. That distinction matters because both terms sound similar to the public, yet they carry different regulatory meanings and levels of evidence.

She also discussed how these approval pathways become more complex when software is involved. Medical devices today are no longer only mechanical or electrical systems; many include software, algorithms, user interfaces, alerts, automation, and data-driven decision support. This makes human factors even more important because failure may not stem from a single broken physical component. It can come from confusing interfaces, poor alarm design, unclear responsibility, incomplete testing, or a mismatch between how engineers expect users to behave and how people actually behave under pressure.

The most memorable part of the session was a case-style discussion about a faulty heart pump. The device had a known failure rate, and the class was asked to think through a difficult question: when a medical device fails, whose responsibility is it? Is it the manufacturer, the designer, the hospital, the clinician, the software team, the regulator, or the system as a whole? That question made the lecture feel very real. In healthcare, responsibility is rarely isolated to one person or one decision. A device can technically meet requirements and still pose risk if the design does not account for human behavior, clinical workflow, training, maintenance, and real-world use conditions.

This discussion changed how I think about engineering design. In manufacturing and product development, it is easy to focus on performance, efficiency, cost, and technical validation. But in safety-critical industries like healthcare, the design must also account for usability, risk perception, decision-making, and failure recovery. A product is not successful just because it works in an ideal test environment. It has to work safely in the hands of real users, in stressful situations, with imperfect information.

Dr. Cha also shared parts of her professional journey, which made the lecture more meaningful. Hearing how human factors can connect engineering, healthcare, research, regulation, and real-world safety helped me better understand the career possibilities within this field. It showed me that industrial engineering is not limited to factories and production systems. The same tools we use to study processes, reduce variation, analyze failure modes, and improve system performance can also be applied to medical devices, healthcare teams, and patient safety.

The biggest takeaway I had from this lecture was that human factors is a responsibility-driven discipline. It asks engineers to think beyond whether a system can function and instead to ask whether it can be used safely, consistently, and responsibly by real people. Whether designing a medical device, a manufacturing process, or a software interface, the goal is not only to improve performance but also to reduce the chance that users are placed in unsafe or confusing situations.

This lecture helped me connect human factors with my broader interest in process improvement, quality engineering, and design validation. It reminded me that good engineering is not only about building systems that work but about building systems that protect people when things do not go as planned.
