Downtime Optimization and Cost Reduction

The System

A busy assembly line where delays, idle machines, people, and utilities all carry cost.

In high-variation manufacturing environments such as automotive and electronics production, process complexity can create production delays, machine downtime, quality issues, and higher unit cost. Our project framed the line as an optimization problem: schedule and sequence tasks so the system loses less time and spends less money.

Model Logic

We translated the production system into decisions, constraints, costs, and sensitivity tests.

The model treated scheduling, station assignment, machine idle state, labor requirements, tooling needs, and task completion time as connected decisions rather than separate symptoms.

What the model decides

The report defined binary and continuous variables for task scheduling, machine working or idle state, people required per task, idle labor, station assignment, tooling requirement, final fitment, assembly time, and delay time.

x[i,j]: task scheduled at time j y[k]: machine working or idle P[i]: people required for task i T[i]: completion time of task i

What the model must respect

Constraints prevented overlapping tasks, enforced delay times, bounded completion time, represented tooling needs, and kept assembly operations feasible. This made the optimization reflect the actual operating rules of a line.

No two tasks can occupy the same time slot Task completion must follow delay logic Assembly time must satisfy minimum process time Final fitment and tool needs are station-dependent

What the model minimizes

The objective function minimized total cost by combining task completion cost, machine idle cost, and idle labor cost. The report also parameterized line breakdown, electricity, compressed air, and manpower costs.

Line breakdown: $11,000 per hour Electricity: $0.30 per kWh Compressed air: $0.10 per cubic mm Manpower: $10 per hour per person

Analysis Story

The project was designed to answer three practical production questions.

Where is idle time most expensive?

Station idle-time sensitivity tested how changes in machine and people idle costs affect total cost. This helped identify which parts of the line deserve the most attention when resources are constrained.

How much do small delays matter?

Delay sensitivity tested the effect of 6- and 7-second delay assumptions on the overall schedule. In repeated production systems, even short delays can compound into meaningful throughput and cost impact.

Which cost drivers shape the decision?

Objective-function sensitivity explored downtime, electricity, and compressed-air cost changes so the team could understand the operational drivers behind the optimized result.

My Takeaway

This project helped me connect optimization theory with manufacturing decision-making. The important part was not just writing equations; it was framing a real production problem so the output could guide scheduling, staffing, downtime reduction, and cost tradeoff decisions.

The project also strengthened my ability to frame an optimization model clearly enough to present, test, and defend in a master's-level industrial engineering course.
