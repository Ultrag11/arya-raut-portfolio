Perceptions of Virtual Reality Training in Manufacturing Environments: Final Report
Samuel Noel, Vishwak Reddy Panyala, Isabelle Peters, Arya Raut
University of Wisconsin–Madison, ISYE 552
This study evaluated the impact of virtual reality training on worker learning using a human factors framework. The goal was to identify where VR supports learning and performance, where it introduces cognitive or physical friction, and how VR training can be improved for manufacturing environments. The study used a two-part approach: a pre-survey to assess perceptions of VR training in automotive manufacturing, followed by an in-person VR task study using an engine assembly/disassembly simulation. During the VR study, participants completed the training task while performance, assistance needs, perceived workload, cognitive load, cybersickness, presence, and physiological data were collected. Performance was measured by task completion time, and the number of questions asked, and subjective workload was measured using NASA-TLX and cognitive load survey responses. ECG data were collected as an exploratory physiological measure, though usable data were limited. Inclusivity and user variability were also considered, as differences in experience, comfort, headset fit, and cue perception can affect the effectiveness of VR training.
INTRODUCTION
Background
Problem Framing & HF/E Significance
Virtual reality (VR) based training is increasingly implemented in manufacturing, as it is a cost-effective way to train new employees. VR can be especially helpful in industries where training can be potentially costly and dangerous, such as the manufacturing of medical equipment, industrial equipment, and automobiles. Despite its advantages, VR has faced resistance due to its inaccurate portrayal of real-life conditions, increased cognitive workload, and the physical discomfort of wearing a VR headset for extended periods. Research suggests VR can improve quality, reduce costs, shorten time to market, and improve user well-being when properly implemented[1]. Instead of basing system success on simple performance and quality metrics, research needs to focus on aligning with human factors principles such as comfort. New VR systems must adjust to workers' cognitive processes while focusing on how workers react to perceptual cues and task demands. This will ensure usability, effectiveness, and continued adoption.
Important stakeholders to consider in the project include the companies paying for the training, and the employees who are being trained. The companies are not only interested in how effectively their employees are trained, but also in applying cost-effective training approaches. VR training offers a cost-effective solution because physical tools are not needed (other than the actual VR headset) and less space is required. In many cases, less staffing support is also needed, which decreases the cost. For employees who are training, VR may allow them to learn in a convenient way, but important considerations must be made to ensure their comfort or learning is not sacrificed.
METHODS
Project Goal
This study evaluated the feasibility of VR-based training for engine assembly and disassembly using a human factors framework. The goal was to identify where VR supports learning, task performance, and user confidence, as well as where it introduces cognitive workload, physical discomfort, or usability barriers. Based on these findings, the study aimed to develop design recommendations that preserve the benefits of VR training while reducing user friction and improving transfer to real-world manufacturing tasks.
User and task analyses overview
A survey was conducted to understand differences between experienced and entry-level manufacturing users, including prior VR experience, current training practices, and attitudes toward VR-supported training. A pre-survey was distributed to participants with relevant automotive manufacturing or engineering experience to identify perceived benefits, concerns, and appropriate use cases for VR training. This approach aligns with prior work evaluating the feasibility of VR in automotive manufacturing contexts [2]. A cognitive task analysis was then conducted with engineering students with limited or no automotive manufacturing experience. These participants were used as a proxy for entry-level workers learning an unfamiliar procedure. Participants completed a step-by-step engine assembly/disassembly task in VR while researchers observed their interpretation of visual cues, written instructions, task prompts, and controller-based interactions. The specific VR program used was an open source engine assembly training program developed by Lan et al at Cornell [3]. During task completion, researchers recorded task duration, questions asked, assistance needs, and qualitative observations. This data helped identify how participants interpreted instructions while completing an unfamiliar task and where instructional design needed improvement. It also revealed common points of confusion, assistance needs, and potential sources of variation during task completion. These insights helped connect the VR task analysis to real-world assembly demands by showing where visual cues, written prompts, controller interactions, or task sequencing created cognitive or perceptual friction. As a result, the task analysis provided evidence for improving onboarding, instruction clarity, feedback, and transfer from VR practice to real manufacturing tasks.

Design concept
For this project, we used the Meta Quest Virtual Reality headset to evaluate how effective VR training is for engine assembly and disassembly. The purpose was to determine whether VR can teach the procedure properly, efficiently, and effectively, and which aspects of the VR design make it easier (or harder) for people to learn the necessary steps to complete the task. This was assessed by attempting to understand feelings towards VR training in automotive manufacturing, and then testing how well the training simulation works in practice.
Human Factors Concepts
Our design and evaluation are guided by human factors issues that often determine whether VR training is adopted in real settings. In particular, we focused on the following concepts: usability, workload, trust, and comfort.
Comprehending usability is vital to understanding the effectiveness of VR training in real-life scenarios. We assessed how easily participants can understand the task at hand by observing how readily they follow instructions and how often they ask for help. Additionally, the workload of these tasks was assessed by measuring cognitive workload using NASA-TLX and ECG data [4][5]. This helps us to understand how much stress this task puts on the body and the mind. Additionally, after our project proposal, a cognitive load survey was added in order to understand what participants found specifically difficult. A cybersickness questionnaire was also added following task completion to understand user comfort, and qualitative observations were recorded.
Perceived trust was also measured both while using the simulation and in the survey sent before testing begins. Trust in the system is of utmost importance to identify any barriers that arise from distrust towards the system. After the task was completed, participants took a presence survey to understand how realistic they felt the simulation was.
Hypotheses
Our first hypothesis was that participants will always need some assistance from observers during task completion, and therefore, the VR training model is not sufficient on its own to adequately train users on the task [6].
The next hypothesis that was tested is that participants who experience cybersickness will show greater perceived difficulty with the task and higher cognitive workload. This was considered important to identify, as someone getting cybersick is something that cannot be controlled, other than allowing breaks [7].
Lastly, we hypothesized that participants with prior VR experience will complete the task  more efficiently and they will show a lower workload[3]. This is important to understand, as the initial survey will identify the level of VR experience among a sample of people from the automotive manufacturing industry [8][9].
Study design
Initially, a survey was sent to automotive manufacturing workers to gauge their attitudes toward virtual reality as a training method. Initially the plan was to get all survey results prior to VR testing, however getting responses proved difficult, so the questionnaire remained open for the duration of all testing.
For in-person VR data collection, participants came into the lab space in the Mechanical Engineering building room 4120 to use the training simulation. This is a large open room that allows for participants to safely interact with the VR without fear of running into obstacles. Participants first filled out a questionnaire aimed at understanding any prior VR experience they had, and then got set up with the actiheart ECG [5]. First participants sat for five minutes without moving or talking to get their resting heart rate, and then training began. Participants spent time training with researchers prior to the task. They were thoroughly instructed on how to use the device and on the common problems that arise during the training simulation. They went through a basic training task to confirm that they understood the instructions. After the introductory training, participants began completing the simulation. The simulation provided both written instructions and visual cues (important areas of the engine were highlighted in green) to indicate what needed to be completed. Researchers observed participants and provided support when unexpected issues arose. The number of questions asked, and specifically what questions were asked was monitored along with qualitative observations. The number of questions asked was especially important in the study, as we aimed to understand how much additional instruction is needed with VR training.
After the task was completed, participants immediately completed a cybersickness questionnaire, NASA-TLX, presence survey, and cognitive load survey [9]. The NASA Task Load Index (NASA-TLX) will be used to measure subjective workload, including mental demand, physical demand, effort, and frustration [4] This allowed us to triangulate both the effectiveness of the training task and the participant experience. The study combined performance data, surveys, and qualitative feedback to understand how participants felt about the VR training. These data points were compared to provide an overall view of VR training in automotive manufacturing. This approach allowed us to evaluate not only how well participants perform but also why certain difficulties occur during the task. These surveys aim to help us understand perceived workload, usability, realism, and overall comfort during the task.

ParticipantsThe study included two participant groups. The first group consisted of experienced participants, such as manufacturing workers or engineers. These participants provided insight into their feelings about virtual reality in automotive manufacturing and any barriers they foresee in a survey that was sent out.
The second group consisted of engineering students, who were important for understanding how well the system itself works and what issues arise. The engineering students are intended to be a representation of a new employee who is potentially unfamiliar with the task but has the necessary mechanical knowledge. These participants were responsible for participating in the actual VR testing.
Consideration of Inclusivity & Variability
Inclusivity must be considered when designing a study. For the purposes of this research, it is important to consider the different groups of people who would use virtual reality training for automotive manufacturing in a real-life scenario. In addition to the different groups of people who would interact with the technology, one must also consider the hurdles people might face that could prevent them from adopting it.
Sex is an important consideration in the study, as females account for roughly 24% of the automotive manufacturing industry [11]. This number continues to increase over time. Females typically have a much higher likelihood of experiencing cybersickness while using a VR headset [12]. Cybersickness is a form of motion sickness that users can experience while using a virtual reality headset. This can cause a headache and nausea. Additionally, the headset's fit must be considered for females. Typically, virtual reality headsets are designed to fit male heads better. It will be important to ensure that the headset is adjusted to fit female users as well as possible, using the top strap to keep the mask up and the dial in the back to tighten the mask to the user’s face [13].
Age must also be taken into consideration. There appears to be inconsistent data on whether age affects the amount of cybersickness experienced; therefore, it must be considered on a case-by-case basis, offering breaks to participants as needed. Age does, however, come into play when considering the training required for users. Older adults often require longer training sessions to fully understand how to use the technology [9]. To ensure all participants understand how to use the technology before use, we will ask them to demonstrate basic tasks and ask if they have any questions. Additionally, participants will be able to ask questions during the use of the technology, but the questions asked will be recorded to understand where confusion commonly occurs during training.
One last barrier that will need to be considered is language or auditory barriers. This includes participants who are unable to hear or do not understand English. To combat this, the program chosen has visual cues such as the necessary parts for the task lighting up in green. This visual clue will indicate how to complete the task. With this in mind, differences in color interpretation between cultures must be considered [14]. To consider this in our trials, we will include questions in the final survey asking participants how they interpreted color cues.
RESULTS
External Survey
As part of the external user analysis, a pre-survey was distributed to individuals with industry or manufacturing-related experience through LinkedIn outreach and cold email. The purpose of the survey was to gauge how potential workplace users perceive VR-supported manufacturing training before conducting the lab-based VR task analysis. Although seven responses were received, one was approximately 25% complete, and another was a test entry, leaving five complete responses for analysis. Given the small sample size, these results were treated as exploratory validation rather than statistically generalizable findings.
Overall, the survey responses suggested that participants were generally receptive to VR training, especially when positioned as a support tool rather than a full replacement for existing training methods. For example, most complete respondents agreed that VR could be a useful addition to training, improve confidence in training, and make complex tasks easier to understand.
Figure 5: Pre-survey validation - receptiveness to VR training.
However, the strongest adoption pattern was not toward replacing traditional training. Four out of five complete respondents preferred VR as a supplement to current training, while one respondent preferred VR only for certain tasks. This indicates that users may accept VR more readily when it is integrated with existing hands-on instruction rather than presented as a standalone training system.
Figure 6: Preferred role of VR in manufacturing training.
The survey also highlighted key barriers to VR adoption. Respondents raised concerns about motion sickness or discomfort, safety, lack of realism, missing real-world details, frustration or inconvenience, and difficulty for some workers. These concerns show that VR training should not only teach the task but also feel realistic, comfortable, safe, and easy to follow.
Figure 7: Key adoption concerns for VR training.
The survey also revealed several concerns that directly informed the design recommendations. Respondents reported motion sickness or discomfort, safety concerns, reduced realism, missing real-world details, and difficulty for some workers. These findings informed the final design recommendations. Because respondents were concerned about realism and the lack of real-world details, the VR system should include more realistic tool interactions, part handling, and physical task replication. Because most respondents preferred VR as a supplement, the system should provide immediate feedback, question support, and opportunities for instructor guidance. Because motion sickness and discomfort were also concerns, VR training should include short breaks, comfort checks, and headset-adjustment support during longer sessions. Finally, because some users may find VR difficult or inconvenient, the instructions should be clearer, more visual, and easier to navigate. Overall, the external survey supports designing VR training as a realistic, comfortable, and user-friendly supplement to current manufacturing training, not as a full replacement.

Presence Survey
A presence survey was conducted as a method of judging VRs performance in aiding participants' training. The questions each represented a specific VR performance metric that was rated 1-7. The data was compiled into figure 13, a heat chart showing the average rating given to each metric. The chart shows good performance for metrics related to user adaption. High scores in VR adaptation speed, event control and action predictability show that users were able to quickly immerse themselves in the training scenario and found they could effectively and efficiently complete tasks given what the VR program provided them. The program struggled, however, to represent the real engine assembly scenario accurately according to participants. They rated interaction realism (3), real-world cohesiveness (3) and movement naturalness (3.1) among the worst performance metrics. In addition to this display quality (3.1) and system latency (2.9) struggled in the eye of participants who claimed they negatively impacted the learning experience as a whole.

A correlation heat chart (figure 13) was made to determine whether any of the presence metrics had relationships with higher completion times or amount of questions asked. High system responsiveness and poor event control serve as primary drivers of performance,  high scores result in users asking significantly less questions and experiencing quicker task completion times. In addition, quickly learning the system through good adaptation speed and visual exploration lead to less questions. Those who struggle to initially understand controls in the VR environment will likely face longer task completion times and more confusion. The high negative correlation between involvement and questions asked shows that when users feel deeply immersed, they are more capable of focusing on their VR surroundings rather than relying on outside verbal cues for guidance. As indicated by the R value of 0.73 and 0.6, interface realism has a strong positive correlation with total questions asked and completion time, respectively. This either indicates the more realistic the surroundings, the harder it is to perform the task, or that participants who were less familiar with engine assembly rated the system more realistic while being confused and performing slower as a result of their inexperience. Interface friction, movement naturalness, and viewpoint flexibility likely suffered a similar fate. The lack of experience and participants led to a positive correlation between better system performance and worse user performance.

Cognitive Load Survey Data
Higher NASA-TLX scores are indicative of high cognitive load experienced over a task. The NASA-TLX scores given by participants after trial completion averaged 42.934 but were highly dependent on the participant as seen with the 18.27 standard deviation. A histogram of NASA-TLX scores separated experienced from inexperienced users to determine the difference between the two groups. Figure 14 shows an  8.233 lower average NASA-TLX score among participants with no VR experience. This would indicate a lower perceived cognitive load among those with no experience when compared to those with previous VR experience. An additional cognitive load survey was conducted to determine for what reason cognitive load was experienced by the participant. The questions fall under two categories: cognitive load undertaken as a result of immersion and learning and cognitive load taken on as a result of interface friction and confusion. When taking this into account, figure 15 and 16 were created to reflect how each of those two categories impacted participants with and without VR experience. Figure 15 shows participants with prior VR experience rated cognitive load to be slightly lower than those with no previous experience, while also rating questions that entailed more involvement in the trial higher (higher germane load). This indicates participants with previous experience to be more involved in the process, showing higher perceived cognitive load mainly in their germane load and NASA-TLX scores.

DISCUSSION
Hypothesis 1
As discussed in the results section, all participants asked at least one question, and averaged 7.7 questions during task completion. The amount of time necessary for task completion correlated to the number of questions asked, and interestingly specifically the number of questions asked in step one. This shows that the amount of confusion in subtask one can be an indicator of how much confusion will be present for the duration of the training.
Interestingly, although participants who asked more questions also had a slightly higher extraneous load, they differed most from participants who asked less questions by having a noticeably higher germane load [16]. These participants who asked more questions than average were more likely to agree that they “tried to understand not only the individual details, but also the overall context”. Specifically, participants 5 and 8 who only asked 5 and 6 questions respectively somewhat disagreed with this statement, while participants 2, 3, and 4 who asked 9, 11. And 10 questions respectively strongly agreed with this statement. This trend was also seen when asked if their “goal was to understand everything correctly while completing this round”. This trend was further emphasized in the presence questionnaire as participants who felt the task was more realistic actually took longer to complete the task. This signals that being more immersed in the learning might actually lead to lower performance measures, but could potentially be more indicative of learning [16].
As researchers, these results indicate that questions are not always indicative of confusion, but instead a desire to fully comprehend and learn. Although the hypothesis that “Participants will always need some assistance from us during task completion, and therefore the VR training model is not sufficient on its own to adequately train users on the task” was true, it emphasizes the need for integrating some form of immediate question response and feedback into the training is beneficial for learning.
Hypothesis 2
While 8/10 participants reported at least one cybersickness symptom, all participants were seen rubbing their eyes after task completion, indicating at least a minor physical response to the VR headset. As more cybersickness symptoms were reported, perceived workload (NASA-TLX) scores also increased. Interestingly, task duration and cognitive load did not increase on average. This provided some diverging results. One potential explanation for the task load not increasing is that perhaps the discomfort acted as negative reinforcement, and encouraged participants to complete the task faster despite their discomfort.
Although we are lacking sufficient physiological evidence to make a strong claim about how cybersickness impacts physical workload, participants 8 and 9 (who had ECG data available) reported high and low cybersickness respectively. When comparing the two, both participants showed similar heart rate changes when compared to baseline, and participant 8 displayed higher RMSSD and LF/HF changes compared to baseline, and therefore more indication of physiological stress when compared to participant 9.
Looking into other metrics, participant 8 also scored higher than average on NASA-TLX, cognitive load, and completion time. This trend was consistent among the top three highest cybersickness reporters (1,2, and 8). Although we cannot fully accept our hypothesis that “Participants who experience VR sickness will report higher perceived difficulty with the task, and show higher cognitive workload” perhaps  this signals that cybersickness can impact performance metrics, but only above a certain threshold (in this case, participants 1,2, and 8 all scored above a 20 on our cybersickness questionnaire). Further research would need to be done with a larger sample size in order to fully comprehend the validity of this statement. Unfortunately the only true way to prevent cybersickness is to allow for participants to take breaks.
Hypothesis 3
Lastly, we explored how prior VR experience impacted users’ ability to complete the task, and if prior VR experience increased performance. Participants with prior VR experience completed the task an average of 1.67 minutes faster, and scored lower on the cognitive load survey, however they reported a higher NASA-TLX score, which was a diverging result when considering workload.
When breaking the questions down of the cognitive load survey, both extraneous load and intrinsic load questions were answered similarly between users with no prior VR experience and with prior VR experience. We would not expect intrinsic load to change, as task difficulty does not change in the experiment. We would potentially expect extraneous load to change with less VR experience as they may have more confusion with the controls, however this was not observed [16]. This suggests promising results that the program itself was user friendly enough for introductory level users, and would not cause too many issues at varying experience levels with virtual reality. Further research would need to be done with users with more experience and a larger sample size to see if this finding would be consistent in a real world application.
Despite this, users with prior VR experience did answer questions in a way that suggested a higher germane load [16]. Specifically participants with more VR experience such as participants 2 and 4 agreed more strongly with the claim that “I tried to understand not only the individual details, but also the overall context” than the participants with no VR experience who all somewhat disagreed with this claim. This suggests that, perhaps, the difference with VR experience is not necessarily a lower workload, but a higher ability to be immersed in the simulation and use it to really learn. Therefore, although we can only somewhat accept our hypothesis that “participants with VR experience will be able to complete the task more efficiently, and will experience lower cognitive workload”, we do believe that participants with prior experience will have a higher germane load during training [16]. This may lead to more learning and comprehension during training.
Design Recommendations
Increased Psychomotor Simulation
Based on these findings, there are a few design recommendations that we suggest in order to improve the implementation of virtual reality for training in automotive manufacturing. First, based on the results of the survey sent to professionals in automotive we want to increase psychomotor simulation in the training. Similar to the technology that is currently being used for training at BMW, we recommend controllers that mimic the shape of the tool, and sense the biomechanical motion of the user to complete the task [17].
In-Training Support and Feedback
Additionally, based on the fact that every single participant asked questions and more questions correlated to a higher germane load, we recommend implementing support options during the task whether that be someone assisting in person, online, or some form of artificial intelligence that can readily clarify any confusing information. In addition to providing clarifying information, these assistants could also provide immediate feedback on whether a task was completed properly, and if there was any way to do so more efficiently.
Increased Instructional Clarity
Next, we recommend improving the clarity of the instructions given to participants. Many of the questions asked by the participants were simply clarifying the instructions that were being given to them, and many of these could have been avoided if the instructions were more clear in the first place. This may include adding images or more description to the instructions.
Encouraged Cybersickness Breaks
Lastly, we want to take into consideration cybersickness because it was a common problem as 8/10 participants reported at least one symptom. Unfortunately, the only suggestion that can be made at this point is to require participant breaks during VR use. With an average completion time of around 15 minutes and most participants having some symptoms, breaks would need to be more frequent, potentially occurring every 10 minutes or less.
CONCLUSION
This two part study looked into how virtual reality can be applied for training in automotive manufacturing. First, professionals from the automotive manufacturing industry were asked to fill out a questionnaire that gauged their feelings on VR training. This yielded 5 complete responses, and participants were generally receptive to the idea of implementing VR, and of these responses, ⅘ expressed that they think VR would be a good tool to supplement training but did not see it functioning independently. Main concerns were realism, missing real-world details, and difficulty for some workers. For the in person study, 10 engineering students acted as participants, completing a VR engine assembly task while wearing an ECG. After task completion, participants filled out surveys to understand cognitive load, presence, cybersickness, and perceived workload (NASA-TLX). The results of this study showed that all participants needed assistance, but more questions asked correlated to a higher germane load, cybersickness correlated to higher perceived workload, and prior VR experience correlated with a higher germane load. Based on all of these findings we recommend increased psychomotor simulation, question and instruction support, and allowing breaks to reduce cybersickness.
